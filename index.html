      Question 3 — Joint and Conditional Probabilities

      Question 3. Joint and Conditional Probabilities [10 points] Let C = “buys coffee weekly” and L = “uses loyalty card.” Given: P(C) = 0.60, P(L) = 0.40, and P(L | C) = 0.50.
      a) Compute P(C ∩ L).
      b) Compute P(C | L).
      c) Interpret both probabilities in a managerial context.

      Tip: Enter as a decimal or percent (e.g., 0.30 or 30).

      Solution

      1. P(C ∩ L) = 0.60 × 0.50 = 0.30.
      2. P(C | L) = 0.30 / 0.40 = 0.75.
      3. Interpretation: 30% do both; among loyalty users, 75% buy coffee weekly.

      Reference: Keller 12e — Ch. 6 (Probability).

      Question 4 — Two-Way Table and Probability Interpretation

      Question 4. Two-Way Table and Probability Interpretation [10 points] Joint probabilities: Dept A & Promoted = 0.12; Dept A & Not Promoted = 0.28; Dept B & Promoted = 0.18; Dept B & Not Promoted = 0.42.
      a) P(Dept A)
      b) P(Promoted)
      c) P(Promoted | Dept A)
      d) Interpret the results for managers.

      Tip: Enter as a decimal or percent (e.g., 0.40 or 40).

      Solution

      1. P(Dept A) = 0.12 + 0.28 = 0.40.
      2. P(Promoted) = 0.12 + 0.18 = 0.30.
      3. P(Promoted | Dept A) = 0.12 / 0.40 = 0.30.
      4. Interpretation: Department A promotion rate equals overall rate (30%).

      Reference: Keller 12e — Ch. 4 (Tables), Ch. 6 (Conditional probability).

      Question 5 — Correlation vs. Causation

      Question 5. Correlation vs. Causation [10 points] Regions with higher advertising budgets tend to have higher sales.
      a) Does this imply causation?
      b) Provide one alternative explanation.
      c) Discuss a business risk of confusing correlation with causation.

      Model Reasoning

      • Does correlation imply causation? No. Correlation alone does not establish cause-and-effect.
      • Alternative explanation(s): (i) Market size: larger regions both spend more on ads and sell more; (ii) Reverse causality: higher sales may lead to higher ad budgets.
      • Business risk: Overspending on ads without incremental lift (wasted budget and opportunity cost).
      • Better evidence: Randomized geo-lift tests, holdout groups, or regression with strong controls.

      Reference: Keller 12e — Ch. 16 (Simple Linear Regression & Correlation).

      Question 6 — Hypothesis Testing for Means

      Question 6. Hypothesis Testing for Means [10 points] Last year’s mean delivery time was μ₀ = 45 minutes with known σ = 6. This year we run a new routing algorithm on a sample of n = 36 deliveries and observe x̄ = 43.5.
      a) State the appropriate hypotheses to test whether the mean decreased.
      b) Compute the test statistic and decision at α = 0.05 (one-sided).
      c) Provide and interpret the p-value.

      Solution

      1. Hypotheses: H₀: μ = 45; H₁: μ < 45.
      2. SE: σ/√n = 6/√36 = 1.
      3. z: (43.5 − 45)/1 = −1.50.
      4. Decision (α=0.05, one-sided): critical z = −1.645; since −1.50 > −1.645, Fail to reject H₀.
      5. p-value: Φ(−1.50) ≈ 0.067 ⇒ not below 0.05.

      Reference: Keller 12e — Ch. 11 (Hypothesis Testing), Ch. 9 (Sampling Distributions).

      Question 7 — 95% Confidence Interval for a Mean

      Question 7. Confidence Interval [10 points] A sample of n = 50 has x̄ = 42 and s = 8. Construct a 95% confidence interval for μ and interpret it.

      Solution

      1. SE = s/√n = 8/√50 ≈ 1.131.
      2. 95% margin ≈ 1.96 × 1.131 ≈ 2.22.
      3. CI ≈ (42 − 2.22, 42 + 2.22) = (39.78, 44.22).
      4. Interpretation: We are 95% confident the true mean lies in this interval.

      Reference: Keller 12e — Ch. 10 (Estimation), Ch. 9 (Sampling Distributions).

      Question 8 — Type I (α) and Type II (β) Errors

      Question 8. Type I and Type II Errors [10 points] We test H₀: μ = 100 at α = 0.05 (two-sided) with known σ = 10 and n = 25. We reject H₀ if |Z| > 1.96. Suppose the true mean is μ = 102.
      a) Define a Type I error in this context.
      b) Compute the Type II error probability β.
      c) Interpret β in managerial terms.

      Tip: Enter β as a decimal or percent (e.g., 0.83 or 83).

      Solution

      1. Type I (α=0.05): Rejecting H₀ when H₀ is true (false positive).
      2. β calculation (μ=102): Acceptance region for x̄ is [96.08, 103.92]. With SE=2, β ≈ Φ(0.96) − Φ(−2.96) ≈ 0.83.
      3. Interpretation: With true mean 102, the test misses the change ~83% of the time (low power).

      Reference: Keller 12e — Ch. 11 (Type I/II), Ch. 9 (Sampling Distributions).

      Question 9 — Statistical vs. Practical Significance

      Question 9. Statistical vs. Practical Significance [10 points] Conversion increases from 8.0% to 8.2% and is statistically significant.
      a) Explain statistical vs practical significance.
      b) Provide a business recommendation.

      Explain the difference and give a manager’s recommendation (briefly).

      Guidance / Solution

      • Statistical significance: p-value small ≠ effect is large.
      • Practical significance: +0.2 pp may be trivial or meaningful; depends on cost and volume.
      • Recommendation: Pilot where ROI is favorable; scale if unit economics justify.
